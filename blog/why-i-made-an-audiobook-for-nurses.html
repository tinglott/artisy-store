Why I Made an Audiobook for Nurses Who Can't Sleep (And What Happened Next)

By Ting Lott, RN — May 12, 2026

Twenty-three years. That's how long I've been a nurse. And for a big chunk of those years, I came home after a 12-hour night shift utterly wrecked — feet aching, shoulders tight, eyes burning — and then stared at the ceiling for two hours while my brain refused to shut up.

If you've ever worked nights, you probably know exactly what I'm talking about. People ask, "Aren't you exhausted after a night shift?" And the answer is: yes, and also, weirdly, no. Your body is running on fumes, but your nervous system is still in full-on alert mode. You're replaying the patient in Room 4. You're wondering if you charted everything correctly. Your heart rate is still slightly elevated from that rapid response call at 4 a.m. You are tired, but you cannot sleep.

This is something the nursing world doesn't talk about nearly enough. We talk about burnout, we talk about short staffing, we talk about compassion fatigue — but we don't always talk about the quiet, exhausting cycle of: work, come home, can't sleep, get a few broken hours, go back to work. Repeat.

The Real Problem With "Why Can't Nurses Sleep After Night Shift?"

I used to Google this exact question. And the articles were... fine. They talked about circadian rhythms, light exposure, cortisol. All true. But nothing really addressed that specific feeling of lying in bed with a racing mind, knowing you need to sleep but feeling completely unable to get there.

The clinical explanation is real: after a night shift, your cortisol levels are often elevated from prolonged stress and vigilance. Your brain has been in "scan for danger" mode all night — which is actually great for patient safety, and terrible for falling asleep. Your body is fighting your biology. It's not a character flaw. It's physiology.

But knowing why it happens didn't help me actually rest.

What I Tried (Before I Found What Worked)

Over the years I tried basically everything the internet suggested for how to wind down after a 12-hour shift.

White noise machines. (Helped a little, not enough.) Melatonin. (Made me groggy the next day, didn't fix the racing thoughts.) Sleep podcasts. (Too much talking — my brain would latch onto something and start analyzing it.) Blackout curtains, chamomile tea, lavender spray. I became a walking sleep hygiene checklist. Nothing quite got me all the way there.

What I needed wasn't information. I needed something to guide my nervous system down. Something that gave my brain just enough to follow — so it would stop chasing its own tail — while being gentle enough not to stimulate me further.

What Actually Worked

I stumbled on it almost by accident. A friend sent me a short guided relaxation recording — not a traditional meditation, just calm, unhurried storytelling with a soothing voice. Something to let my imagination follow, softly, like being walked slowly into a quiet room.

For the first time in years, I fell asleep within twenty minutes of a night shift.

I started collecting recordings like this. Seeking them out. And slowly I realized: there was nothing specifically designed for nurses. Nothing made by someone who understood the particular weight of what we carry out of that building at 7 a.m.

So I made it myself.
