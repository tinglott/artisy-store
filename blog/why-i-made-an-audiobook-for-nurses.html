Twenty-three years. That's how long I've been a nurse working night shifts. And for twenty-three years, I haven't slept properly.

Not because I didn't want to. Not because I wasn't exhausted. But because after spending twelve hours on your feet, managing crisis after crisis, holding people's hands through their worst moments—your body crashes, but your nervous system stays locked in overdrive.

I'd walk through my front door at 8 AM, utterly spent. My eyes would burn. My legs would ache. Every muscle screamed for rest. But the second my head hit the pillow? My brain wouldn't stop. Racing thoughts. Adrenaline still pumping. That post-shift buzz that won't quit.

The Problem Nobody Talks About

If you're a nurse, you know exactly what I mean. Why can't nurses sleep after night shift? It's because we're trained to be hyper-alert. To catch problems. To respond instantly. Night shifts train your nervous system to stay activated—and then we expect ourselves to just flip a switch and rest.

I tried everything. White noise machines? They made it worse—my brain would fixate on the hum. Melatonin supplements? Felt artificial, left me groggy the next evening. Podcasts? Most are either too engaging or aggressively boring. I was stuck in that awful space between exhaustion and wakefulness, watching the clock tick toward afternoon—too late to sleep, too tired to do anything useful.

My colleagues felt the same way. We'd swap tips in the break room like we were sharing trade secrets. "Try a weighted blanket." "I take a hot bath first." "Just accept you won't sleep." That last one haunted me. We'd normalized sleep deprivation as just part of the job.

What Actually Worked

Not nature sounds.
