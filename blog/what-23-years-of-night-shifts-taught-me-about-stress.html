What 23 Years of Night Shifts Taught Me About Stress (And How I Finally Found Peace)

By Ting Lott, RN | Artistrystore.com

It was 4:47 a.m. on a Tuesday. I'd just finished double-checking medications for a patient in Room 7, answered three call lights, and updated two charts — all while running on three hours of sleep from the day before. The hallway was finally quiet. I leaned against the nurses' station counter and thought, I don't know how much longer I can do this.

And then the phone rang again.

That's the moment I think about when people ask me how night shift nurses deal with stress. The honest answer? Most of us don't — not well, anyway. We absorb it, carry it home in our scrubs, and collapse into bed where our brains replay every decision we made for the last twelve hours.

The Stress Nobody Talks About

Everyone knows nursing is stressful. That part isn't a secret. But what people outside the profession don't understand is the specific kind of stress that comes from night shift work. It's not just the hours. It's the constant switching — your body is on one clock, the world runs on another, and your family needs you somewhere in between.

Can nurses have work-life balance? People used to laugh when I asked that. After 23 years, I'll tell you: yes — but it doesn't happen by accident. And it doesn't come from pushing harder.

The stress I carried wasn't just physical exhaustion. It was emotional weight. The patient I couldn't stabilize fast enough. The family member sobbing in the hallway at 2 a.m. The colleague who quit last month and left us short-staffed again. I was bringing all of that home, and I didn't even realize it until my kids started saying, "Mom, you look far away."

How do you stop bringing work stress home when you've been doing it for two decades? I had to answer that question for myself before I could answer it for anyone else.

What Finally Helped Me

I tried a lot of things. Exercise — great in theory, impossible at 8 a.m. after a twelve-hour shift when your body doesn't know what time zone it's in. Journaling — helpful, but I'd stare at a blank page and not know where to start. I even tried just watching TV to "decompress," but I'd fall asleep mid-show and wake up more disoriented than before.

What actually worked was slower. Quieter. It started with mindfulness — not the trendy app version, but real, intentional pausing. Learning to notice when my nervous system was in overdrive. Learning to breathe on purpose instead of just surviving.

Writing helped too. Not venting — processing. There's a difference. Venting keeps you in the story. Processing helps you step back and see it. I started writing short reflections after hard shifts — not long essays, just a few honest lines. Over time, I noticed I was sleeping better. My shoulders weren't permanently up near my ears. I was present with my kids again.

What helps nurses sleep after night shift isn't always a supplement or a sound machine (though I still love my white noise). Sometimes it's what you do with your mind before you ever lie down.

You Deserve More Than Survival

Twenty-three years taught me a lot about nursing. But the most important thing I learned wasn't a clinical skill — it was this: you cannot pour from an empty cup, and you are not selfish for refilling yours.

We show up for everyone else, always. We miss holidays and bedtimes and family dinners. We hold hands through the worst moments of people's lives. And we do it willingly, because we care. But caring for others doesn't mean abandoning yourself.

If you're reading this at 7 a.m. after a long night shift, still wound up, still carrying the weight of the last twelve hours — I see you. I was you. And I want you to know that peace is not out of reach. It just might look a little different than you expected.

Rest is not a reward. It's a right. You've earned it a thousand times over. 💜
