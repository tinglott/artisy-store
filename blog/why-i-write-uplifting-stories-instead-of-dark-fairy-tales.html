Why I Write Uplifting Stories Instead of Dark Fairy Tales (And Why Our Kids Need More Light)

I've been a nurse for 23 years. I've sat beside people in their hardest moments — held hands, stayed late, witnessed things that would break most people's hearts wide open. And I'll be honest with you: the world can be a really heavy place.

So when I come home — when I peel off my scrubs and sit on the couch with a cup of tea — the last thing I want to do is read my child a story filled with darkness, fear, and cruelty. There's enough of that out in the real world. What I want, what I believe every child deserves, is a story that lifts them up.

That's why I write uplifting stories for children. And that's exactly what I want to talk about today.

Why the World Needs More Uplifting Stories Right Now

If you ask why uplifting children's stories are important, my answer is simple: because children absorb everything. They are sponges — emotionally, mentally, creatively. The stories we give them don't just entertain. They become part of the lens through which kids see the world.

Study after study shows that stories help children with anxiety by giving them emotional vocabulary, safe frameworks for processing big feelings, and — crucially — hope. When a child sees a character face a challenge and come out the other side stronger, something shifts inside them. They think: maybe I can do that too.

That's not a small thing. That's everything.

And yet so many classic fairy tales — the ones we've handed down for generations — are laced with fear. Abandoned children, evil stepmothers, wolves in the dark, punishments for curiosity. I understand their history. I really do. But I kept asking myself: what if we told these same stories through a lens of hope instead of dread?

What Inspired Me to Start Writing My Own Stories

It started with my own kids. I would read them bedtime stories and sometimes find myself quietly editing on the fly — softening a cruel line here, adding a word of encouragement there. I wanted them to hear about courage, kindness, and resilience, not just terror and darkness.

Then, as a nurse, I started noticing something in the families I worked with. So many kids were anxious. Overwhelmed. Struggling to feel safe in an uncertain world. And I kept thinking about the power of a good story — not as escapism, but as a genuine tool for healing and growth.

So I sat down and I started writing. Late nights after shifts. Early mornings before the house woke up. I wrote the stories I wished existed.

Classic Fairy Tales — Reimagined With Hope

Once Captain Davie found his voice, I turned back to those classic stories that had always bothered me. What if I could retell them — keep the magic and the wonder — but fill them with light instead of fear?

That's how Hansel & Gretel: A Story of Hope and The Snow Queen: A Modern Magical Tale were born.

Are there children's books about hope and resilience? Now there are. My versions of these beloved classics aren't sanitized to the point of being toothless — there's still adventure, tension, stakes. But the heart of each story beats with courage, love, and the belief that things can get better. Because they can. They always can.

In my Hansel & Gretel, two siblings find not just survival but strength in each other. In The Snow Queen, the magic isn't something to fear — it's a mirror for inner transformation. These are stories that give children the emotional tools to face their own difficult moments.
